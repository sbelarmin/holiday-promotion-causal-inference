# Executive Summary  
## Causal Evaluation of 40% Promotional Campaign

### Objective

Assess whether a 40% discount campaign generated incremental margin dollars.

Primary outcome: **Margin dollars**  
Secondary outcome: Revenue  

The campaign was observational and targeted — not randomized.

---

## Key Finding

After adjusting for customer mix and seasonal effects, the promotion reduced margin by approximately:

### **–$4.6 per exposed user-day**

At campaign scale, this represents a material short-term margin loss.

---

## What Happened

- Units increased during the promotion.
- Revenue increased modestly.
- Margin rate declined sharply.
- Net incremental margin impact was negative.

The discount depth overwhelmed incremental volume gains.

---

## Credibility of Results

We tested the effect using multiple methods:

- Naïve comparison
- Difference-in-Differences
- Two-Way Fixed Effects
- Propensity Score Weighting
- Weighted DiD
- Event Study diagnostics

Results were consistent across specifications.

Parallel trends diagnostics show no major pre-period divergence.

---

## Important Caveat

The campaign was highly targeted toward higher-value customers.  
Statistical reweighting methods (IPW) were limited due to lack of overlap.

However, dynamic event study analysis supports the DiD estimate.

---

## Business Implication

The promotion appears economically destructive in the short term.

To justify the strategy, long-term benefits would need to exceed:

- Short-term margin losses
- Discount-driven profitability erosion

Without evidence of sustained retention or LTV gains, the discount depth should be reconsidered.

---

## Recommendation

Evaluate:

- Lower discount depth (e.g., 20–25%)
- More targeted margin-protected segmentation
- Long-term customer retention impact

Short-term economics alone do not support the 40% strategy.
